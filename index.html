What Shape Is a Box Made of Primes?

Consecutive integers build predictable boxes. Consecutive primes build wilder ones. The difference reveals something deep about irregularity itself.

Lee McCulloch-James · Dwight School London · 2025

SCROLL TO EXPLORE

The simplest interesting box

Take the integers 1, 2, 3 as sides of a box. You get a cuboid — volume 6, shape slightly elongated. Now take 1, 2, 3, 4 and build in four dimensions. Keep going. In n dimensions, the box has sides 1 through n and its volume is n!

This is the oblong cuboid: the ancient idea of oblong numbers (products of two consecutive integers, dating to the Greeks) promoted to arbitrary dimension. Its volume is a factorial. Its surface encodes the harmonic series. And it turns out we can measure its shape exactly.

But what happens if we use consecutive primes instead? Sides 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, … — a box we'll call a puboid. Same question. Wildly different answer. And the gap between the two reveals a structural divide that reaches all the way to quantum field theory.

Factorials have Stirling. Primorials have … nothing.

Isolating shape from size

The surface-to-volume ratio S/V is the obvious shape measure — but it's scale-dependent. Double every side and S/V halves. We need a quantity that sees only the proportions, blind to absolute magnitude. The isoperimetric quotient does exactly this:

Isoperimetric Quotient for Cuboids
M = SV(n−1)/n = 2n · GH

G is the geometric mean of the side lengths; H is the harmonic mean. The factor 2n is just the reference value for a perfect hypercube (all sides equal). Everything interesting lives in G/H.

G/H = 1 means perfect symmetry (a hypercube). G/H > 1 means the sides are unequal — more "stretched." Larger G/H = more asymmetric. This is guaranteed by the AM–GM–HM inequality: H ≤ G ≤ A, with equality iff all values coincide.

Our question becomes concrete: how does G/H grow with dimension n, and how does the answer change when we swap integers for primes?

Integers: the exact answer

For the oblong cuboid (sides 1, 2, …, n), both means have classical asymptotics rooted in 18th-century analysis. Stirling's approximation gives G = (n!)1/n ~ n/e. The harmonic series gives H ~ n/log n. Their ratio:

Oblong G/H — Exact Asymptotics
GH ~ log ne
Clean. Closed-form. The Euler–Mascheroni constant γ ≈ 0.577 governs corrections.

Shape asymmetry grows logarithmically — slowly, predictably, exactly. The constant γ = −Γ′(1) connects this geometry to the gamma function and thereby to physics: dimensional regularisation in QED, Feynman integrals, analytic continuation. The shape of a box built from integers speaks the same language as quantum field theory.

Primes: where the theory breaks

For the puboid (sides p₁, p₂, …, pₙ), we bring serious machinery. Chebyshev's theorem controls the geometric mean via the first Chebyshev function θ(x) ~ x. The Meissel–Mertens theorem handles the harmonic sum of reciprocal primes. First-order prediction:

Factor of error

Off by a factor of three. This isn't a rounding error — it's a qualitative failure. The culprit: the approximation θ(pₙ) ~ pₙ hides a slowly vanishing error εₙ that enters as an exponent. Since G = exp(θ(pₙ)/n), a small additive error in the exponent becomes a multiplicative error in G itself. Terms that are individually negligible compound devastatingly through exponentiation.

This is the fundamental obstacle: there is no "Stirling's approximation for primorials." Factorials admit clean asymptotic expansions because integers are uniformly spaced. Primorials resist them because primes are irregularly distributed. The error is negligible — until you exponentiate it.
A negligible error, exponentiated, becomes a factor of three.

The mysterious coefficient C(n)

Since exact asymptotics fail, the data suggests an empirical law: G/H ≈ C(n) · log pₙ. The coefficient C(n) increases slowly and has no known closed form. Here it is, computed directly:

C(n) = (G/H)pub / log pn

Two worlds

The oblong and puboid are governed by parallel constants playing analogous corrective roles. Yet their mathematical universes are fundamentally different.

Oblong Cuboid

G/H ~ log(n) / e
  • Stirling gives exact coefficients
  • Euler–Mascheroni constant γ ≈ 0.577 corrects
  • γ = −Γ′(1) — appears throughout physics
  • Reflects continuous, additive structure

Puboid

G/H ≈ C(n) · log pn
  • No "Stirling for primorials" exists
  • Meissel–Mertens constant B₁ ≈ 0.261 corrects
  • B₁ has no known physics applications
  • Reflects discrete, multiplicative structure

The constants are connected:

Connection
B₁ = γ + Σp prime [ log(1 − 1/p) + 1/p ]

Both correct leading asymptotic terms. But their universality differs profoundly. γ appears in every branch of physics involving regularisation. B₁ never does. This isn't coincidence — it's structural.

Why primes don't do physics

Four structural reasons why B₁ (and prime distributions generally) are absent from physics:

Continuous Symmetry

Physics is built on Lorentz, gauge, and diffeomorphism symmetries — all continuous. Prime distributions are discretely irregular, with no natural Lie group structure.

Density

QED regularisation sums over all momentum states. Primes are too sparse (pₙ ~ n log n vs integers growing as n) to serve as a regularisation scheme.

Multiplicative vs Additive

B₁ arises from unique factorisation — fundamentally multiplicative. Conservation laws (energy, momentum, charge) are additive. The structures don't match.

No Analytic Continuation

Γ(s) extends factorials; ζ(s) extends integer sums. Primorials and Σ(1/pᵢ) lack clean analytic continuations, so they can't appear in regularisation.

γ is universal. B₁ is specific.
That is why physics uses one and ignores the other.
Open Problem

What is C(n)?

The empirical coefficient C(n) ≈ 0.7 in G/H ≈ C(n) · log pₙ increases slowly and has no known closed form. Can it be expressed via the logarithmic integral Li(x), Riemann's R(x), or the error term of the Prime Number Theorem?

Specifically: is there an analytic relationship between C(n) and the remainder pₙ/π(pₙ) − log pₙ? The shape geometry of prime-sided boxes would then be directly governed by the distribution of primes — a concrete geometric manifestation of the PNT's irreducible error.

Further Directions

Beyond primes

What G/H growth rates arise from twin primes, primes in arithmetic progressions, or values of multiplicative functions? The isoperimetric framework applies to any positive sequence — different growth rates would reflect different distributional properties, potentially revealing new geometric signatures of arithmetic structure.
