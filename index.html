Number Theory × Geometry

What Shape Is a Box Made of Primes?

Consecutive integers build predictable boxes. Consecutive primes build wilder ones. The difference reveals something deep about irregularity itself.

Lee McCulloch-James · Department of Natural Science, Dwight London · 2025

SCROLL TO EXPLORE

The simplest interesting box

Take sides 1, 2, 3. You get a 3D box — a cuboid — with volume 6 and a particular shape. Now take sides 1, 2, 3, 4 and build in 4D. Keep going. In n dimensions, the box has sides 1 through n and volume n!

This is the oblong cuboid: an ancient idea (oblong numbers go back to the Greeks) promoted to higher dimensions. Its volume is a factorial. Its shape encodes the harmonic series. And it turns out we can measure its shape exactly.

But what happens if instead of consecutive integers, we use consecutive primes? Sides 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, … — a puboid. Same question, wildly different answer.

Factorials have Stirling. Primorials have … nothing.

Isolating shape from size

The surface-to-volume ratio S/V is the obvious measure — but it's scale-dependent. Double every side and S/V halves. We need something that cares only about the proportions of the side lengths, not their absolute size.

Isoperimetric Quotient for Cuboids
M = SV(n−1)/n = 2n · GH

Here G is the geometric mean and H the harmonic mean of the side lengths. The factor 2n is just the reference value for a hypercube (all sides equal). Everything interesting lives in the ratio G/H.

G/H = 1 means perfect symmetry (a hypercube). G/H > 1 means the sides are unequal — more "stretched". Larger G/H = more asymmetric shape. This is guaranteed by the AM-GM-HM inequality.

So our question becomes concrete: how does G/H grow with dimension?

Integers: the exact answer

For the oblong cuboid (sides 1, 2, …, n), both means have classical asymptotics. Stirling's approximation gives G = (n!)1/n ∼ n/e, while the harmonic series gives H ∼ n/log n. The ratio:

Oblong G/H — Exact Asymptotics
GH ∼ log ne
Clean. Closed-form. Euler–Mascheroni constant under the hood.

Shape asymmetry grows logarithmically — slowly, predictably, exactly. The Euler–Mascheroni constant γ ≈ 0.577 governs the correction terms, and γ = −Γ′(1) connects to the gamma function, which is why it appears throughout physics: QED regularisation, Feynman integrals, dimensional analysis.

Primes: where the theory breaks

For the puboid (sides p₁, p₂, …, pₙ), we bring serious machinery. Chebyshev's theorem controls the geometric mean via θ(x) ∼ x. The Meissel–Mertens theorem handles the harmonic sum. First-order prediction:

Naïve Prediction
GH ∼ log log n
At n = 1000 this predicts ≈ 1.93

The actual value at n = 1000? 6.07. Off by a factor of three. The approximation θ(pₙ) ∼ pₙ hides sub-leading terms that compound devastatingly through the exponential in the geometric mean.

This isn't a small correction — it's a qualitative failure. The slowly vanishing error εn in θ(pₙ) = pₙ(1 − εₙ) gets exponentiated, amplifying it from negligible to dominant. There is no "Stirling's approximation for primorials."
The error is negligible — until you exponentiate it.

What the data actually says

Since exact asymptotics fail, we look at the data empirically. The puboid G/H turns out to be approximately linear in log pₙ:

Empirical Law
GH ≈ C(n) · log pn
where C(n) ≈ 0.7, increasing slowly

Compare with the oblong formula G/H ∼ log(n)/e where 1/e ≈ 0.368. The prime coefficient is roughly 1.9× larger — primes stretch boxes almost twice as aggressively as integers do.

Two worlds

Oblong Cuboid

G/H ∼ log(n) / e
  • Stirling gives exact coefficients
  • Euler–Mascheroni constant γ corrects
  • γ = −Γ′(1) — appears throughout physics
  • Reflects continuous, additive structure

Puboid

G/H ≈ C(n) · log pn
  • No "Stirling for primorials" exists
  • Meissel–Mertens constant B₁ corrects
  • B₁ has no known physics applications
  • Reflects discrete, multiplicative structure

The constants γ and B₁ play parallel roles — correcting leading asymptotics in their respective domains. They're even related:

Connection
B₁ = γ + Σp prime [ log(1 − 1/p) + 1/p ]

But their universality differs fundamentally. γ appears in every branch of physics that involves regularisation. B₁ never does. This isn't an accident — it reflects the deep incompatibility between prime distributions and continuous symmetries.

Why primes don't do physics

Four structural reasons why B₁ (and prime distributions generally) lack physical applications:

Continuous symmetry. Physics is built on Lorentz, gauge, and diffeomorphism symmetries — all continuous. Prime distributions are discretely irregular. There is no natural Lie group whose structure constants involve primorials.
Density. QED regularisation sums over all momentum states. Primes are too sparse to serve as a regularisation scheme without missing most of the physics. You can't build a path integral from prime-indexed modes.
Multiplicative vs additive. B₁ arises from unique factorisation — fundamentally multiplicative. Conservation laws (energy, momentum, charge) are additive. The mathematical structures don't match.
No analytic continuation. The gamma function Γ(s) extends factorials. The zeta function ζ(s) extends sums. Primorials ∏pᵢ and Σ(1/pᵢ) lack clean analytic continuations — so they can't naturally appear in regularisation procedures.
γ is universal. B₁ is specific.
That's why physics uses one and ignores the other.
Open Problem

What is C(n)?

The empirical coefficient C(n) ≈ 0.7 in G/H ≈ C(n) · log pₙ increases slowly and has no known closed form. Can it be expressed via the logarithmic integral Li(x), Riemann's R(x), or the error term of the Prime Number Theorem?

Specifically: is there an analytic relationship between C(n) and the remainder pₙ/π(pₙ) − log pₙ? If so, the shape geometry of prime-sided boxes would be directly governed by the distribution of primes — a concrete geometric manifestation of the Prime Number Theorem's error.
